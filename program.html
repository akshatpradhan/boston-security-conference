1. Lean security: a practical approach to security projects

By adapting key ideas from Agile Development and Lean Manufacturing, this talk illustrates a smarter way to strategize security. Learn how to think differently and create a cost-conscious, less-is-more security environment. Stop locking horns and get your team to work together by focusing on learning, simplicity and measurement. It's time to shift from Best Practice to constantly improving Better Practice.

Josh believes that security works best from a holistic approach, and works all angles: risk assessments, posture analysis, incident response, malware analysis, infrastructure defense, system forensics, employee training and business strategy.

2. Blitzing with your defense

The days of simply responding to alerts are over and a shift to employing more "active" defenses along with developing intelligence about threat actors has started. 4. Top 10 web defenses

We cannot “firewall” or “patch” our way to secure websites. In the past, security professionals thought firewalls, Secure Sockets Layer (SSL), patching, and privacy policies were enough. Today, however, these methods are outdated and ineffective, as attacks on prominent, well-protected websites are occurring every day. Organizations around the world rely on highly accurate, scalable and continuous Web security services to maintain the safety of their websites in today’s hostile online environment. This talk will discuss the 10 most important security-centric computer programming techniques necessary to build low-risk web-based applications. Real-world tradeoffs will be discussed in detail for each "control" and "control category" discussed.

5. Stacking to find unknown evil

“Am I compromised now?” At Mandiant, we are often tasked with helping our clients answer that question. To do this effectively, we have to be able to find “unknown evil” across enterprises consisting of tens of thousands of systems. One method we use is called stacking. To do this, we collect information about hosts in the target environment (installed services, running processes, directory listings, etc.) and “stack” this data to identify anomalies and find traces of previously unknown attacker activity. In this talk, we will walk you through how we do this, using real world examples where we identified attacker activity that evaded standard detection mechanisms implemented by our clients.

6. Social engineering: understood through the lexicon of classic swindling

In this talk, a bridge is built between the contemporary understanding of highly technical Social Engineering and the long standing art of the swindle and confidence game. There is particular emphasis put on the specialized argot of the turn of the century American confidence man as documented by David W. Maurer. The overall objective of the brief is to show that the world of Internet based Social Engineering can and should be described, categorized, and understood trough existing taxonomies of classical grift techniques and language.

7. Introduction to mobile application security assessments

With the surge of mobile devices accessing not only external web applications, but also internal company systems, organizations are increasingly worried about the potential risk for data breach and data loss that exists. While many packaged solutions have been developed to combat the risks, organizations face budget constraints and a lack of experienced staff to implement these solutions. Companies are increasingly turning to application security assessments of mobile solutions to reduce their exposure to these risks. This talk will introduce the viewer to the concept of a mobile application security assessment, how it differs from traditional web or network assessments and dive into the tools and techniques auditors use when searching for security issues on mobile devices.

8. Security in a connected vehicle system

The US Department of Transportation is considering mandating a system for inclusion in all light vehicles that would allow them to broadcast their position and velocity on a more-or-less continuous basis. The system is claimed to have the capability to prevent up to 80% of all unimpaired collisions. We discuss the security needs, the constraints due to cost and other issues, and the efforts that are being made to ensure that the system will not compromise end-user privacy.
